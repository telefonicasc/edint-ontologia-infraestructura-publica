Infraestructure: Description return to Table of contents

Figure 1 captures the general conceptual model of the infrastructure ontology.

The public and private elements of a municipality’s infrastructure (infrastructure element) include the (sub)classes urban element —such as traffic lights or pedestrian crossings— and facility. Facilities include educational, social, cultural, and sports facilities, as well as on‑street and off‑street parking areas and shared bicycle stations. Geospatial concepts from the GeoSPARQL ontology are reused to represent areas or coordinates of infrastructure elements by using the concept gsp:Feature and the property gsp:hasGeometry. The elements belong to an administrative unit (Municipality, District, Neighborhood), which are concepts from the Territorial Division Ontology, and have a postal address

In addition, infrastructure elements have an infrastructure type, which is a concept belonging to a SKOS taxonomy, such as “Health Center,” “Cultural Center,” “Parking,” etc. An infrastructure element may offer regular activities —for example, a cultural center may host concerts, exhibitions, etc. It may also provide public services, such as Wi-Fi access. The infrastructure element has roles played by organizations. Roles can have a type, such as security or maintenance, which are concepts belonging to a SKOS taxonomy. In turn, the role is made on an infrastructure element. Infrastructure elements are also related to the organizations that own them (has registered owner). One facility may contain other facilities. This relationship allows representing different areas within a facility. For example, a cultural center may have auditoriums and exhibition halls; a sports center may contain a football field or a swimming pool.

The infrastructure ontology reuses sensor-related concepts from the (Semantic Sensor Network Ontology) to represent access and counts of people and vehicles at any infrastructure element, as well as vehicle traffic measurements. An infrastructure element may have observation points. An observation point is a feature of interest of observations made by sensors, which in turn observes an observable property such as vehicle speed, traffic density, or the access of a vehicle or a person. Figures 2, 3, and 4 illustrate the reuse of this ontology for access sensors, counting sensors, and traffic measurement devices, respectively.

Figure 2 shows concepts related to access sensors.

In the case of access observations, these are performed by access sensors for an observable property called access. Access observations may include the vehicles they detect and can be used to document a violation, which will be linked to a vehicle and will have a violation type —this type being a concept belonging to a SKOS taxonomy, such as speeding, mobile phone use, etc.

Figure 3 shows concepts related to counting sensors.

In the case of counting observations, these are performed by counting sensors for an observable property called facility occupancy.

Figure 4 shows concepts related to traffic sensors.

For traffic-related sensors, concepts from the Ontology for the representation of vehicle traffic data are reused. Specifically, this ontology includes traffic observations, traffic measurement devices, and traffic measurement properties. The observable properties traffic occupancy, level, intensity, and speed can be reused. The property traffic density has been added as a new observable property.

Sensors of any of the three types considered in the ontology (access, counting, and traffic) have a manufacturer, which is an organization.
